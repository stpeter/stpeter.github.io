Complete Thyself: Chapter 4

CHAPTER 4: LOVING

4.1 TAKING RELATIONSHIPS SERIOUSLY

[the importance of sociality: love, friendship, and fellowship suffuse all of our interactions, our every moment, our entire way of life, for we are made for living with people in a community, and nothing brings as much joy as another person; these relationships are not only necessary but beautiful; humans are social not in the way that, say, bees are, since we are *selectively* social: by choice we form bonds with, make commitments to, and form fellowships with particular people who evoke in us a feeling of inherent kinship; this is especially true of close, lifelong pairings of the sort celebrated among the ancients (such as Achilles and Patroclus in the Iliad and Odysseus and Penelope in the Odyssey); even among family members we have preferences and favorites; you can be especially close to only a few people in life because such a relationship requires a significant investment of time and attention; (move this down to 4.4) the doing right that happens in private within our personal relationships is up to us and thus matters more for character than legislated justice and public custom; love is less an external good than a shared project, which is why it has mutual meaning and deep internal significance]

4.2 FORMS OF LOVING

[relationships based on enjoyment, usefulness, and character; relationships based on character are the most stable and long-lasting and beautiful because reciprocal commitment and traits of character are relatively immutable, whereas what a person finds enjoyable or useful can change with the circumstances, with the stages of life, and with the different roles we play in society; the centrality of mutual caring, and for caring about the person for their own sake vs experiencing pleasure unmoored from shared activities or gaining some good thing that has no natural limit (honor, wealth, power, status, strength, health, etc.); relationships based on character are complete (having all three properties of being good, useful, and enjoyable) whereas the others are incomplete (only useful or only enjoyable); people who take life seriously seek out relationships based on character; at the same time, someone who is dear to you is not only good plain and simple, but good to you and for you by your own lights; equality vs superiority in various respects (e.g., flattery of a superior person provides merely a semblance of friendship, compared to true friendship rooted in equality), and even in character relationships equality of excellence is important; marriage, family, friendship, brotherhood; family systems and their inherent deformations, of which Aristotle demonstrated little awareness]

4.3 LOVING AS AN ACTIVITY

[love as a feeling vs loving as an activity; it is not enough to feel positively disposed to the other person and wish the other person well: you need to act on those feelings and wishes; the activity of loving is better than the experience of being loved or receiving admiration and esteem, for these experiences are relatively passive and don’t involve activity - and living simply is activity; furthermore, the activity of loving has no goal beyond itself (use or pleasure) but is an end in itself; (dup, move this up?) a relationship is a kind of shared project that requires significant time, care, and intimacy to blossom through likeminded commitment, reciprocal recognition, common goals, cooperative activity, and mutual attention; the connections among caring (epimelein), tending (therapeuein), and attending (theorein); what’s entailed in shared living (shared days, shared doings, shared feelings, shared pains, shared pleasures, shared character, a shared way of life - all those ‘sun-‘ words which in English could have a ‘with-‘ prefix); the particular activities depend on your conception of what life is all about (eating, drinking, hunting, playing dice, making music, seeking wisdom, etc.); in relationships of character, sharing a way of life consists of working together on the tasks of living, thriving, minding, loving, knowing, and being; how the activity of loving in a character relationship integrates and provides a foundation for enjoyment and advantage, giving them a meaning and stability they don’t have on their own; the inherent pleasures of living in company with others; shared living as a celebration of human capacities and meaningful activities, which is especially so for the beautifully good person who takes life seriously]

4.4 THRIVING IN RELATIONSHIPS

[how the thrivings are brought to bear in interpersonal relationships (e.g., courage, moderation, generosity, justice in the sense of not doing each other wrong - with wisdom and discernment suffusing all); loving as an activity that both incorporates and extends beyond these thrivings in a steadfast way that can be trusted and relied upon; managing the pleasures and pains of human relationships; in the paradigmatic relationship, the guiding force is not merely a feeling but a mutual commitment to the good and to each other, founded in stable traits of character; loving as itself a form of thriving because those who love each other collaborate in making things go well, doing good works, and taking beautiful actions for their own sake (not to be repaid in kind); loving as an outlet for the overflowing excellence of the beautifully good person who takes life seriously: it is fulfilled, seemingly independent people who can truly seek out the beautiful actions of interdependence because they come to love and friendship not out of need but from a surplus of loving and giving]

4.5 LOVE AND WHOLENESS

[relationships as a source of self-completion and wholeness (autarkeia is not self-sufficiency or independence, but interdependent wholeness and mutual emulation of the good, especially in family and close friendships but also in less intimate fellowships and community relations); the meaning of Aristotle’s image of the loved one as “another self”, whose primary sense comes from parenting and biological reproduction, especially a mother’s love for her child; the activity of loving is not a merely external good but dwells inside you and thus transforms you into a better person; the integration of the pleasant, the useful, and the good in a complete relationship supports internal integration and helps you steer clear of self-indulgence; self-completion leads to self-knowledge through articulation of shared ideals and the practice of speaking freely (parrhesia) and setting each other straight in search of mutual improvement; in relationships rooted in character, the two people are aware together and know together; this knowledge is not something separate from living but deeply integrated into your way of life, suffusing your doings and feelings with knowing and awareness so that not only do you know yourself but you yourself are a knower; living is a kind of knowing, which is why fulfillment extends as far as attention does]

4.6 THE CARE OF THE SOUL

[Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle on epimeleia tes psuches: caring for the soul, cultivating personhood, attaining harmonious integration, achieving a unified self, transforming what is good plain and simple into what is good for yourself; being a friend to yourself happens only when you achieve harmony among the parts of the soul; what’s right and what’s wrong about self-love; the Euthyphro argument applied to self-love; the nature of the loveable; love of the good, aspiration toward the beautiful, and living in truth as the basis for relationships of character and for self-love in the proper sense; love of self is love of the good and the beautiful in oneself; yet it is difficult to know if you are beautifully good, and you can avoid the lure of self-deception only through truthful self-assessment and the honesty of close relationships; the self-directed growth of your self-knowledge and self-completion is a lifelong task]
