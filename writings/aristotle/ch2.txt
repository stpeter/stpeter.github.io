CHAPTER 2: CHARACTER

2.1 TAKING GOODNESS SERIOUSLY

The first aspect of life to take seriously is goodness: the potential to raise yourself above a merely animal way of life by activating your highest human capacities for action and emotion, doing and feeling, aspiration and exaltation.

Some people are naturally good in certain ways: for instance, they might not be prone to anger, jealousy, intemperance, or fear; they might tend to be mild, generous, kind, or brave. Yet these qualities, which nowadays we would call personality traits, aren’t always balanced or reliable, and can even get you into trouble; we are all familiar with people who are generous to a fault or who are so mild-mannered that they don’t become indignant at manifest injustices.

For Aristotle, more balanced and reliable than these inborn tendencies are acquired traits of character. Traditionally these have been called virtues and vices, but I render the Greek terms as thrivings and failings. Thrivings are more balanced because you actively direct them based on your understanding of the human condition, and they are more reliable because you intelligently adjust and apply them in particular circumstances.

As a first approximation, goodness can be achieved by steering clear of extremes in your actions and reactions (recall the Delphic maxim “nothing to excess”). Aristotle draws an analogy to archery: your arrows can either fall short of the target or overshoot it. Thus for each thriving there are not one but two kinds of failing: for example, the courageous person finds a middle range between cowardice and rashness, while the generous person finds a middle range between wastefulness and stinginess.

However, merely avoiding extremes doesn’t necessarily get you where you want to be. Hitting the target in action and emotion is a matter of identifying and enacting what is appropriate or fitting in a given situation, and this entails more than simply “too much” or “too little.” Goodness has many aspects: doing and feeling at the right time, with regard to the right kind of person, to the right extent, with the right frequency, for the right reasons, etc.

Thus Aristotle argues that, for instance, apparent bravery in battle isn’t true courage if a soldier behaves that way in routine situations, toward non-combatants, out of proportion to the current threat, in ignorance of the dangers he faces, or because he’s trying to avoid a court-martial. Similarly, a truly generous mother would help her child in ways that are attuned to her child’s current needs (e.g., for moral instead of financial support), the history of their relationship, and her goals for her child’s development (e.g., to encourage the child’s eventual independence).

Even beyond what’s appropriate or fitting is what’s beautiful. Although this way of characterizing goodness is typically Greek, even we on occasion might say to someone “that was a beautiful thing you did.” Identifying what makes an action beautiful is not straightforward, since there is an aesthetic element involved; yet in such actions we can perceive elements of grandeur, significance, harmony, fluency, style, proportion, discernment, devotion, seriousness of purpose, and a special degree of fit to the occasion.

Although people who take goodness seriously might not consistently attain what is admirably appropriate or beautifully right, their commitment at least is to aspire to that height of action and emotion.

2.2 PLEASURES AND PAINS

Aristotle ties all the thrivings and failings of character to how you handle the pleasures and pains of life. Whereas with our materialist mindset we moderns tend to think of pleasure and pain in physiological terms, Aristotle applies the terms to a wide range of experiences and emotions. For instance, he observes that human beings rejoice at acquiring wealth and bemoan its loss; similarly, we experience anger as distressing and fear as disturbing. Naturally, he also recognizes the more bodily pleasures we derive from the senses of smell, taste, and touch, which he associates with those aspects of life that we have in common with other animals.

Moreover, Aristotle holds that for human beings all activities have their own inherent enjoyments, along several dimensions. One dimension is carnal vs. spiritual: for instance, the joy of sex differs from the joy of having a meaningful conversation with the one you love and the joy of basking in the sun differs from the joy of understanding solar physics. A second dimension is active vs. passive: for instance, the joy of cooking differs from the joy of eating and the joy of making music differs from the joy of listening to it.

One of the dangers of pleasure is that human beings can all too easily pursue for its own sake, unmoored from the underlying activity. Instead of simply cherishing the times when we get to break bread with our friends, we become fixated on the ever more refined and expensive pleasures of gourmet dining; instead of celebrating social occasions with a glass of wine, we drown our sorrows in binge drinking; instead of taking quiet satisfaction in prudently planning for retirement, we thrill to gambling on risky investments.

Aristotle argues that pursuing pleasure for its own sake inevitably leads you astray since it corrupts your character and warps your understanding of human life. By contrast, being content with the inherent joy of an activity preserves your good character and reinforces your insights into the human condition.

2.3 SOULCRAFT

Aristotle wrote three books with "ethics" in the title: the Nicomachean Ethics, the Eudemian Ethics, and the Great Ethics (or Magna Moralia). To our ears, “ethics” brings to mind commandments, guardrails, and other restrictions on behavior. Yet because the Greek word for ethics is derived from the Greek word for character, I prefer to talk about soulcraft. Whereas the political art of statecraft pertains to the public, communal world of societal governance, the ethical art of soulcraft pertains to the private, personal world of cultivating your highest self.

At the same time, one of Aristotle’s most frequently quoted observations is that human beings are social animals. This is why so many character traits and their associated activities are concerned with community commitments (e.g., courage), social interactions (e.g., moderation), and interpersonal relationships (e.g., love).

More precisely, Aristotle says that humankind wavers between social and solitary. The fact that we tend in both directions is partly why the human condition is a matter of such high complexity. Because Aristotle characteristically does justice to this complexity, his account of soulcraft goes beyond the modern dichotomy between individualism and altruism. In our most meaningful personal relationships, each person cares about and acts on behalf of the other for their own sake and because of who they are. Thus although the goal of life to complete yourself, a completely solitary life is not complete: a person can’t wholly flourish without flourishing friends, family, and fellow citizens.

And yet, just as you can’t be financially giving if you haven’t first built up the wealth that makes free-hearted generosity possible, so also you can’t be giving in your relationships if you haven’t built up the character that makes love, friendship, and brotherhood possible. As Socrates argued at his trial in Plato’s Apology, soulcraft is the source of true wealth in life.
