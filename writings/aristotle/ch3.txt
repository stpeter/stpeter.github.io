Complete Thyself: Chapter 3

CHAPTER 3: WISDOM

3.1 WISDOM AS A THRIVING

As with the other thrivings, wisdom too finds a middle path, in this case between foolishness and cunning. The wise person combines the kind of beautifully good character we’ve just talked about with beautifully good judgment; by contrast, the foolish person falls short of wisdom by being of basically good character but exercising poor judgment, whereas the cunning person overshoots wisdom by making clever decisions but lacking the moral compass provided by having good character.

The wisdom that Aristotle has in mind is not the serene detachment of a sage or saint, but the active know-how portrayed for all time in the Homeric hero Odysseus. The wise person isn’t merely a good thinker, but someone who demonstrates their knowledge in action and in their long-term commitments.

Another ancient paragon of action-oriented wisdom was Pericles, the longtime leader of Athens at its height, who had a thoroughgoing awareness of what was good for himself and for human beings in general: it’s true that he did well individually, but he also helped the Athenians to flourish as a people.

Because practical know-how entails a significant degree of cleverness, if unmoored from good character it can easily shade over into mere cunning. Thus true wisdom is additive: your decision-making powers can’t be beautifully good if you lack the foundation of a beautifully good character.

What makes for beautifully good judgment? Aristotle approaches the question from two ends of the spectrum.

At its most immediate, good judgment requires excellent discernment of what’s feasible in the moment; this is an almost perceptual ability to identify the best course of action right now, often based more on experience than on knowledge or reasoning. Yet mere experience can take you only so far: the person who acts without deliberation and deeper insight might often be lucky in hitting the mark, but is liable to go astray in more challenging circumstances or even succumb to unthinking convention.

At its most rarified, good judgment requires a comprehensive understanding of the highest good achievable in action, i.e., the best life for human beings. It is in terms of this that Socrates insisted his friends and fellow citizens should provide an account for their way of life. Following in the Socratic tradition, Aristotle distinguished between living as directed by the results of correct reasoning (quite possibly someone else’s reasoning) and living in such a way that all of your activities are suffused with thoughtful reflection. Only in the latter case are you able to spell out why you live the way you do, experience complete inner harmony, and truly live with purpose.

3.2 A LIFE WORTH LIVING

Aristotle asks a question that must strike us as strange: what is the task or job or work of a human being? Consider also a related question that seems to have been common among the ancient Greeks: if you were given the choice, why would you choose to have been born rather than not?

To answer these questions in modern terms, we would have to say: a person’s task is not merely to be successful in a particular line of work but to be successful as a human being, because it is only by finding true fulfillment that you can craft a life which is preferable to the alternative of having never been born.

Although these stark statements might seem somewhat exaggerated, we can use them to trigger reflection into what makes life worth living. For Aristotle, at its best a life of true fulfillment meets the following criteria.

First, it’s complete unto itself: it’s not pursued for the sake of something else (say, health or wealth), but is prized for its own sake; indeed, good things such as health and wealth exist for and contribute to the more complete state of overall fulfillment.

Closely related is a second point: a life of fulfillment has a kind of wholeness, in that it lacks nothing of significance which would make a noticeable difference if missing, such as friends and community.

Third, it’s not a grind limited to the necessities, but is organized around more free, beautiful, glorious activities such as festive meals, intelligent conversation, aesthetic appreciation, and loving relationships.

Fourth, it’s enjoyable, even blissful.

It’s no coincidence that for the ancient Greeks these criteria applied first and foremost to the Homeric gods, who were everlastingly young, healthy, and gorgeous, didn’t have to concern themselves with earning a living, and could treat life as one endless festival.

Humans, by comparison, had a sorry, short-lived lot (thus the wonder about whether it’s better not to have been born at all). Yet the Greeks thought that even we mortals can share in the divine and attain some measure of fulfillment by focusing on the higher things in life and on the activities that make life worth living. As Aristotle put it, we should strain every nerve to reach upward and as much possible to live like the immortals.

This best life - not grovelling for money, power, status, or fame - is our highest human purpose.

3.3 ADJUSTING YOUR AIM

That all sounds well and good in theory, but in practice how do we build such a life and pursue the highest good achievable in action?

Consider the months-long task of building a house. Here everything is done for the sake of the completed house, and the architect or builder understands how and why each part and activity contributes to that overarching goal. Yet, comparatively speaking, building a house is a relatively simple affair. Completing the years-long task of crafting a life well lived is a much more complex and intricate endeavor, for there’s no set of architect’s plans to follow, the “materials” of life aren’t standardized, most of us don’t have a clear idea of what our aspirations should be until we’re halfway through the construction process, and we’re continually issuing change orders to ourselves as the site shifts beneath our feet.

Even worse, many people go through life like philosophical day-laborers, unaware that constructing a lifelong state of fulfillment is a worthy or feasible goal in the first place. And yet it is just that goal which functions as an organizing principle for your life, which bestow upon your day-to-day activities a sense of structure, a measure of meaning, the wonder of oneness, and even the grace of grandeur. Indeed, fulfillment is not a state that is delayed until the end of life, but is an ongoing enactment of your highest capacities and aspirations.

The challenge is transforming this high purpose into everyday action by closing the gap between wish and fact, intention and commitment, ideal and real. Aristotle has some powerful suggestions for doing so, which surprisingly come from his foundational studies of logic.

Step one is to consciously set this universal purpose as your personal target in life, as the underlying premise for all of your choices and decisions. After all, if you’re not aiming at the right target, you’ll never hit it! The analogy with archery is serious business because in ancient Greece that skill was not a mere hobby but a matter of life and death both in hunting and in the courageous defence of family, friends, and community.

For Aristotle, it is the beautiful goodness of your character that makes your target the right one. However, you also need to know how to hit the target in particular situations, which is the role of deliberation.

3.4 LIVING DELIBERATELY

One of the distinctive capacities of human beings is that we have a deep sense of the future. From his extensive biological research, Aristotle recognizes that certain other animals have natural abilities analogous to human recollection, know-how, craft, comprehension, and intelligence. However, he thinks that only human beings live deliberately, i.e., engage in long-term deliberation, forethought, and planning. Thus if we are to succeed at being human, we need to take deliberation seriously.

The scope of deliberation depends on the timeframes and levels of abstraction involved. Deliberating about what to cook for dinner tonight differs from deliberating about how to eat more nutritiously in the coming year, which differs from deliberating about whether to pursue a career as a nutritionist, which differs yet again from deliberating about the relative importance of health in relation to other commitments such as personal relationships and earning a living. As these horizons widen, so too do the factors that come into play, up to and including your plans for your entire life.

Fundamentally, deliberation is a kind of search or inquiry. Guided by an overall goal, you take counsel with yourself and narrow down your options to identify the activities and commitments that best enable you to make progress in the direction of your chosen target - which, we hope, is the best human life you can imagine.

Aristotle likens the process of deliberation to a logical proof: you start with the premise that you want to live a life well lived and iteratively unfold or thicken that conception until a somewhat indeterminate ideal is transformed into a determinate action. When the conclusion of your deliberations becomes inseparable from and one with your chosen target, you see your purpose in your activity of the moment and you see your activity of the moment as an instantiation of your purpose. This simultaneous “seeing in” and “seeing as” is a practice of being minded in a special way. Not only do you imagine the possibility of a beautiful way of life, but you perceive the actuality of beautiful particulars in your projects, relationships, and other commitments.

We moderns are accustomed to thinking that a specific activity is merely a means to end. Aristotle, by contrast, enourages you understand that a specific activity simply is your purpose as enacted in this moment.

When your purpose is thus transformed into action, you experience psychic unity, inner harmony, and a seamless integration between your past, present, and future.

3.5 DEMONSTRATING COMMITMENT

[beyond specific activities and choices lies the realm of true commitment; wisdom and the virtues; is there only one virtue?]

- doings and feelings are the “matter” of commitment, whereas the telos is the form: what we need to do is bring form and matter together, and this is what deliberation does by transforming or shaping the telos into a determinate praxis
- the object of deliberation is made determinate in the object of commitment by means of discernment (κρινεῖν)
- a commitment is an active stance of the soul, like the tensing of a bow
- when you truly instantiate the thrivings, you take action in such a steadfast way that you don’t lose your equilibrium if circumstances change (1105a31ff)
- what you commit to do is something within your reach that you think through and yearn for: once you discern the best course of action, your yearning becomes well-defined because it is directed by your resolution to take action, and (barring akrasia) the resolution in turn guides your action to a successful consummation

3.6 WISDOM IN ACTION

[maintaining awareness, avoiding akrasia; situational thinking and practical truth; action with deliberation and action with immediate insight; maintaining awareness of the telos in the praxis is what enables you to avoid akrasia]

- awareness is present both in the activity of deliberation and in the action that issues from a deliberative commitment; the awareness is both constitutive and directive of the activity of living (see adjectival eidos at 1105a31)
- thriving involves the whole person; this is seen most prominently in free-heartedness, great-heartedness, and justice - but beyond them also in wisdom and love
