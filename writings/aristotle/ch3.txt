Complete Thyself: Chapter 3

CHAPTER 3: WISDOM

3.1 WISDOM AS A THRIVING

As with the other thrivings, wisdom too finds a middle path, in this case between foolishness and cunning. The wise person combines the kind of beautifully good character we’ve just talked about with beautifully good judgment; by contrast, the foolish person falls short of wisdom by being of basically good character but exercising poor judgment, whereas the cunning person overshoots wisdom by making clever decisions but lacking the moral compass provided by having good character.

The wisdom that Aristotle has in mind is not the serene detachment of a sage or saint, but the active know-how portrayed for all time in the Homeric hero Odysseus. The wise person isn’t merely a good thinker, but someone who demonstrates their knowledge in action and in their long-term commitments.

Another ancient paragon of action-oriented wisdom was Pericles, the longtime leader of Athens at its height, who had a thoroughgoing awareness of what was good for himself and for human beings in general: it’s true that he did well individually, but he also helped the Athenians to flourish as a people.

Because practical know-how entails a significant degree of cleverness, if unmoored from good character it can easily shade over into mere cunning. Thus true wisdom is additive: your decision-making powers can’t be beautifully good if you lack the foundation of a beautifully good character.

What makes for beautifully good judgment? Aristotle approaches the question from two ends of the spectrum.

At its most immediate, good judgment requires excellent discernment of what’s feasible in the moment; this is an almost perceptual ability to identify the best course of action right now, often based more on experience than on knowledge or reasoning. Yet mere experience can take you only so far: the person who acts without deliberation and deeper insight might often be lucky in hitting the mark, but is liable to go astray in more challenging circumstances or even succumb to unthinking convention.

At its most rarified, good judgment requires a comprehensive understanding of the highest good achievable in action, i.e., the best life for human beings. It is in terms of this that Socrates insisted his friends and fellow citizens provide an account for their way of life. Following in this Socratic tradition, Aristotle distinguished between living as directed by the results of correct reasoning and living in such a way that all of your activities are suffused with thoughtful reflection. Only in the latter case are you able to spell out why you live the way you do and thus truly live with purpose.

3.2 A LIFE WORTH LIVING

[the purpose of a human being; the examined life; awareness; action meta logou; giving an account of how you live; spelling things out]

fulfillment is not a state that is delayed until the end of life, but is an ongoing energeia: being fulfilled now implies having been fulfilled

- a purpose not transformed into action is not really a purpose; what transforms a purpose into an action is deliberation that results in a commitment; thus for the flourishing person telos, skopos, deliberation, commitment, and action are a seamless whole, and are suffused with logos
- it is your conception of human fulfillment that gives structure and definition to your day-to-day activities (these are aspects of beauty)
- to give an account (“logon didonai”) is to spell out why your life is the way it is, or more specifically why you’ve lived as you have done; this is a quest for understanding and meaning (Arendt)
- if you have lived well or at least aim to live well (if your target is completion as a human being), then giving an account involves an understanding of human nature and a beautiful way of life (you take action “meta logou” in the fullest sense); this entails educating and pruning your desires toward the end of lifelong fulfillment as opposed to temporary pleasures
- another aspect of action “meta [orthou] logou” is doing the right thing for the right reasons, unlike the Spartans (EE 1248b36-1249a3) who do the right thing for the wrong reasons; examples of the wrong reasons include wealth, power, pleasure (subjective well-being, happy feelings), even health
- this kind of reasoning treats things that by right are ends in themselves (love, friendship, knowledge, etc.) merely as means to a less worthy end
- phronesis entails taking action for reasons you have internalized and made your own; in part this is the difference between action kata logon and action meta logou because your praxis is itself a way of demonstrating your understanding of why you live and act the way you do
- living and acting meta logou means that your understanding of life completely suffuses your day-to-day activities - this is wholeness, integration, harmony

3.3 ADJUSTING YOUR AIM

[transforming a human being’s purpose into your target; understanding the best for a human being of things done in action; beautiful goodness makes your target the right one, but you also need to know how to hit the target in particular situations, which is the role of deliberation]

The analogy with archery is serious business because in ancient Greece that skill was not a mere hobby but a matter of life and death in the courageous and skillful defence of your family, friends, and community.

- in the example of building a house, everything is done for the sake of building the completed house (PA 639b14ff etc.) and the architect or builder understands how and why each part and activity contributes to that goal; yet building a life is a much more complex and delicate endeavor

3.4 LIVING DELIBERATELY

One of the distinctive capacities of human beings is that we have a deep sense of the future. From his extensive biological research, Aristotle recognizes that certain other animals have natural abilities analogous to human recollection, know-how, craft, comprehension, and intelligence. However, he thinks that only human beings live deliberately, i.e., engage in long-term deliberation, forethought, and planning. Thus if we are to succeed at being human, we need to take deliberation seriously.

First and foremost, deliberation is a kind of search or inquiry. Guided by an overall goal, you take counsel with yourself and narrow down your options to identify the activity that best enables you to make progress in the direction of your chosen target. Indeed, deliberation helps you to identify that direction in the first place, and to give greater specificity to your conception of a life well lived. Deliberation the everyday form of your quest to live the best life within your reach.

The scope of deliberation depends on the timeframe involved. Deliberating about what to cook for dinner tonight differs from deliberating about how to eat more nutritiously in the coming year, which differs yet again from deliberating about whether to pursue a career as a nutritionist. As the timeframe widens, so too do the factors that come into play, up to and including your plans for your entire life.

[the interplay of forethought and reflection; the practice of being minded]

Perception of the beautiful particulars requires not just knowledge but insight, which is the task of wisdom as the intellectual thriving of thinking things through and deliberating well about your commitments and activities. We turn to that topic next.

[nous as seeing the abstract in the particular; what wisdom understands; clarifying your conception of human fulfillment]

[thinking as thickening your conception of life, turning a somewhat indeterminate ideal into a determinate action]

- when the conclusion of your deliberations becomes inseparable from and one with the telos, you *see* the telos *in* the praxis and you see the praxis as an instantiation of the telos: this “insighting” is nous
- another way of putting it is that you are committed to the praxis for the sake of the telos: the praxis simply *is* the telos in this kairos (which is why after the “thickening” process there is no longer any distance between telos and praxis); thus it is misleading to speak of “means” and “ends” as separate things
- yet this can happen in the moment, too, especially as you gain more experience in life
- deliberation pertains to what is “up to us” and what is within your *reach*
- one aspect of practical truth is having an accurate assessment not only of the situation but also of your own abilities

3.5 DEMONSTRATING COMMITMENT

[beyond specific activities and choices lies the realm of true commitment; wisdom and the virtues; is there only one virtue?]

- doings and feelings are the “matter” of commitment, whereas the telos is the form: what we need to do is bring form and matter together, and this is what deliberation does by transforming or shaping the telos into a determinate praxis
- the object of deliberation is made determinate in the object of commitment by means of discernment (κρινεῖν)
- a commitment is an active stance of the soul, like the tensing of a bow
- when you truly instantiate the thrivings, you take action in such a steadfast way that you don’t lose your equilibrium if circumstances change (1105a31ff)
- what you commit to do is something within your reach that you think through and yearn for: once you discern the best course of action, your yearning becomes well-defined because it is directed by your resolution to take action, and (barring akrasia) the resolution in turn guides your action to a successful consummation

3.6 WISDOM IN ACTION

[maintaining awareness, avoiding akrasia; situational thinking and practical truth; action with deliberation and action with immediate insight; maintaining awareness of the telos in the praxis is what enables you to avoid akrasia]

- awareness is present both in the activity of deliberation and in the action that issues from a deliberative commitment; the awareness is both constitutive and directive of the activity of living (see adjectival eidos at 1105a31)
- thriving involves the whole person; this is seen most prominently in free-heartedness, great-heartedness, and justice - but beyond them also in wisdom and love
