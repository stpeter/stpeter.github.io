Aristotle on ἀγαπᾶν

translated by Peter Saint-Andre

The following passages constitute the occurrences of ἀγαπᾶν and related terms in the works of Aristotle.

14b6

... what is better [βέλτιον] and more valued [τιμιώτερον] is thought to be prior by nature: ordinary people commonly say of those they specially value [ἐντιμοτέρους] and cherish [ἀγαπωμένους] that they 'have priority'.

291b27

We regard zeal [προθυμία] as a proof of modesty rather than over-confidence, if one whose thirst for philosophy leads him to cherish [ἀγαπᾷ] even minor solutions when faced with a significant impasse.

645a14

(Ogle modified) Indeed, it would be strange if we enjoyed mimic representations of them (i.e., living things) because they disclose the mimetic skill of the painter or sculptor, but we didn't treasure [ἀγαπῷμεν] even more the natural realities, being able to discern the causes.

731b2

But the function [ἔργον] of the animal is not only to generate (which is common to all living things), but they all of them participate also in a kind of knowledge [γνῶσις], some more and some less, and some very little indeed. For they have sense-perception, and this is a kind of knowledge. (If we consider the value [τὸ τίμιον] of this we find that it is of great importance compared with the class of lifeless objects, but of little compared with the use of the intellect [τὸ φρονεῖν]. For against the latter the mere participation in touch and taste seems to be practically nothing, but beside plants and stones it seems most excellent; for it would seem a treasure [ἀγαπητόν] to gain even this kind of knowledge rather than to lie in a state of death and non-existence.) And it is by sense-perception that an animal differs from those organisims which have only life.

980a23

All human beings naturally yearn to know. A sign of this is that we treasure [ἀγάπησις] the forms of awareness; for even apart from their usefulness, they are treasured [ἀγαπῶνται] for themselves - and especially sight.

1076a15

We must be content [ἀγαπητόν] if we can state some points better, and others no worse.

1094b9

... for even if it (the good) is achieved for only one person that is something to be satisfied with [ἀγαπητόν], but to do so for a people [ἔθνει] or a community [πόλεσιν] is more beautiful and divine.

1094b19

So one must be content [ἀγαπητόν] to point out the truth in broad brush and in outline ...

1095b17

... for this reason they cherish [ἀγαπῶσι] a way of life centered on enjoyment...

1096b11

... the good things spoken of as coming under one way of being are those pursued and cherished [ἀγαπώμενα] for their own sake ...

1118b4

It is the most common forms of awareness to which dissipation applies, and it seems this is most justly reproached, because it is present not insofar as we are human beings but insofar as we are animals. To delight in such things and especially cherish [ἀγαπᾶν] them is beastlike...

1119a19

One who would go that far cherishes [ἀγαπᾷ] such pleasures beyond their worth [ἀξίας], whereas the moderate person does not do so like that, but as the correct account [ὀρθὸς λόγος] would have it.

1120b13

... all people cherish [ἀγαπῶσι] more the results of their own efforts, as parents and poets do.

1167b32-1168a8

... there truly is loving [φίλησις] in the case of those who have thoroughly shown that they love [φιλοῦσι] and cherish [ἀγαπῶσι] people, even if they aren't useful now or in the future. In this happens with craftsman, for each of them cherishes [ἀγαπᾷ] their own work more than the work would do if it were to come alive. And perhaps this happens especially with poets, since they have too much love [ὑπεραγαπῶσι] for their own poems, being fond of [στέργοντες] them like children.... The reason is that ... the work is in a certain way the maker at-work, so that they are fond of [στέργει] the work because they are fond of being.

1168a29

One might raise an impasse whether one ought to show the most love toward oneself or someone else; for people criticize those who especially cherish [ἀγαπῶσι] themselves...

1168b33 - 1169a3

Just as a community [πόλις], or any other organization, seems especially to be its most authoritative part [τὸ κυριώτατον], so too does a human being; and so the person who cherishes [ἀγαπῶν] and gratifies this is especially a lover of self. And people are called self-indulgent and self-restrained according to whether their insight [τὸ νοῦν] masters them or not, as if this were each person; and it seems that the things people have done themselves and done willingly are especially suffused with reason [μετὰ λόγου]. That, then, this is each person or is especially each person, is not unclear, and nor that a humane person [ἐπιεικὴς] especially cherishes [ἀγαπᾷ] this.

1171a20

It is not possible to be a friend to many people on account of virtue and for themselves, and one must be content [ἀγαπητόν] to find even a few such friends.

1171b29

Is it then the same as with lovers, for whom seeing their beloved is what they cherish most [ἀγαπητότατον]?

1175a13

Living is a certain kind of activity, and each person is active about and by means of those things which he especially cherishes [ἀγαπᾷ] ...

1177b1

And it (theorein) seems to be the only one (activity) cherished [ἀγαπᾶσθαι] for its own sake.

1179a22-29

One who is actively directed by insight and cultivates [θεραπεύων] this and so is best ordered would seem to be most loved by the gods; for if some care [ἐπιμέλεια] for things human comes from the gods, as is believed, then it would be reasonable for them to delight in what is best and what is most akin to them (and this is insight) and to act favorably in return especially toward those who cherish [ἀγαπῶντας] and honor this most, as such people care for [ἐπιμελουμένους] what they love and act rightly and beautifully.

1179b18

... perhaps one should be content [ἀγαπητόν] if, when everything is present by which we seem to become decent, we might gain a share of virtue.

1180a14

... these thinkers also say that the pains inflicted ought to be of the sort that are most opposed to what people treasure [ἀγαπωμέναις].

1181a21

... for those who lack experience, we must be content [ἀγαπητόν] if they do not fail to notice whether the work is well or badly made, as in the case of painting.

1233b2

... the wedding of a loved one [ἀγαπητοῦ] ...

1240a33

... it's a mark of cherishing [ἀπαπᾶν] someone to share in their suffering ...

1262b23

There are two things which most of all make human beings feel care and love: what is their own [τὸ ἴδιον] and what they cherish [τὸ ἀγαπητόν].

1307a18

People call governments that incline more toward oligarchy aristocracies, and those that incline more toward the multitude constitutional governments. This is why the latter sort are more secure than the former, since the greater number are stronger, and they are more content [ἀγαπῶσιν] when they have an equal share.

1348a29

... the Lycians cherished [ἀγαπῶντας] wearing their hair long ...

1365b16-19

(Roberts modified) That which is treasured [ἀγαπητόν] is better than what is not - the sort of thing that some people have only one of, though others have more like it. Accordingly, blinding a one-eyed man influcts worse injury than half-blinding a man with two eyes; for the one-eyed man has been robbed of what he treasured [ἀγαπητόν].

1371a21

It is pleasant to be loved, for this makes you see yourself as having goodness, which is craved by all beings with awareness; to be loved is to be cherished [ἀγαπᾶσθαι] for what you are in yourself.

1398a23

(quoting) "Everyone will agree that the immoderate person is one who is not content [οὐχ ἀγαπᾶν] with taking pleasure in only one body."

1420b12

Just as a painless life is preferred, so is wise reason something to cherish [ἀγαπητός].
