Aristotle on εὐδαιμονίζειν

translated by Peter Saint-Andre

The following passages constitute the occurrences of the term εὐδαιμονίζειν in the works of Aristotle.

1096a2

(Sachs modified) [I]t seems possible while having virtue, even to be asleep or to be inactive [ἀπρακτεῖν] throughout life, and on top of these things, to suffer evils and the greatest misfortunes. No one would call someone who lived that way 'fulfilled' [εὐδαιμονίσειεν].

1100a9-16

(Sachs modified) Children are called 'blessed' due to our hopes. For there is need, as we said, of both complete virtue and a complete life [βίος], for many changes and all sorts of changes come about in the course of a life [κατὰ τὸν βίον], and it is possible for the most thriving person [εὐθηνοῦντα] to fall into great misfortunes in old age, just as the story is told of Priam in the epics about Troy; no one calls 'fulfilled' the one who suffers such misfortunes and dies in misery. But then one ought to call no other human being 'fulfilled' who is still alive, and it is necessary, as Solon said, to look to the end [τέλος ὁρᾶν]? But if one really ought to lay down this rule, is anyone fulfilled [εὐδαίμων] once he is dead? Or is this completely absurd, both for other reasons and especially for us who say that fulfillment [εὐδαιμίαν] is some sort of activity [ἐνέργειαν]? But if we do not say that someone who is dead is fulfilled [εὐδαίμονα], and Solon did not mean that, but that only at that time may one safely call a human being 'blessed' [μακαρίσειεν], as being then beyond evils and misfortunes, this too has in its something disputable....

1101b24-28

(Sachs modified) [T]here is no praise for the highest goods, but something greater and better, as is also evident since we call the gods, as well as the most godlike among men, 'blessed' [μακαρίζομεν] and 'fulfilled' [εὐδαιμονίζομενον]. And it is the same with good things, for no one praises fulfillment [εὐδαιμονίαν] as one would praise justice, but rather blesses it [μακαρίζει] as something better and more divine [θειότερον].

1176b12

... but many of those who are called happy [εὐδαιμονιζομένων] escape into pastimes of this sort ...

1219b7

... a person cannot be fulfilled only for a day, or when a child, or at every stage of life; thus it was beautifully said by Solon to call no one 'fulfilled' [while still alive [τὸ μὴ ζῶντ᾽ εὐδαιμονίζειν], but only once his life has reached completion [λάβῃ τέλος], since nothing incomplete [ἀτελὲς] is fulfilled [εὔδαιμον], for it is not whole [ὅλον].
