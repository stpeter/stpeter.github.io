Aristotle's Great Ethics

Book I, Chapter 25

Translated by Peter Saint-Andre

Great-heartedness is in the middle range between being conceited and being small-hearted. It pertains to being well regarded and poorly regarded, specifically about the regard that comes not from the many but from those who take life seriously — for they honor people based on knowledge and accurate discernment. Thus the great-hearted person will want to be honored by those who know, just as he does, that he is worthy of honor. For neither does he care about just any kind of regard but about the best, and about the kind of glorious good that ranks as a source of other goods.

Those are conceited indeed who are contemptible and worthless yet think themselves worthy of great things and deserving of high esteem. By contrast, those are small-hearted who think they deserve less than befits them. Thus the person between these two is the one who neither thinks he deserves less esteem than he deserves or befits him, nor thinks he deserves all kinds of esteem or more esteem than he is worth. This then is the great-hearted person, and great-heartedness is in the middle range between being conceited and being small-hearted.
